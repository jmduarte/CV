Javier M. Duarte is an experimental high energy physicist, who studies the basic building blocks of matter at the highest energies producible in the laboratory, and develops machine learning (ML) techniques and hardware-software systems to enable this research.
Duarte is a member of one of the large experimental collaborations at the CERN Large Hadron Collider (LHC) in Geneva Switzerland consisting of several thousand scientists called CMS.
Such a large collaboration is needed to build the complex detector, operate it in the extreme conditions of the LHC beam, acquire the data from the collisions, and analyze the data to infer the properties and interactions of the subatomic particles produced in the collisions.
Nonetheless, Duarte is recognized as an intellectual leader within the field due to his contributions advancing Higgs boson studies and real-time ML for triggering and data acquisition.
The major research question that Duarte seeks to answer is what is the nature of the \emph{new physics} beyond the standard model of particle physics, the current best description of the elementary particles and the fundamental forces.

The direction Duarte pursues is to study the most recently discovered elementary particle, the Higgs boson, to see if its properties deviate from expectations, as this may provide crucial hints as to the nature of the new physics.
His research in CMS currently focuses on searches for and measurements of Higgs bosons decaying to quarks with large transverse momentum ($\pt$).
By studying the production of Higgs bosons at high $\pt$, Duarte's work is sensitive to new physics at very high energy scales.
In addition, Duarte searches for Higgs boson pair production in order to measure the Higgs boson's interactions with itself and other particles.

Within CMS, he established the high-\pt, merged $\PH\to\bbbar$ decay channel as one of the most sensitive experimental probes of Higgs boson production~[\mycite{Sirunyan:2020hwz}, \mycite{Sirunyan:2017dgc}, \mycite{CMS:2022nmn}, \mycite{CMS:2026znu}].
He, along with collaborators, also developed a new algorithm based on a transformer~[\mycite{Li:2024htp}, \mycite{CMS:2026uph}] to better identify the Higgs boson decay in a myriad of channels, including $\bbbar$, $\Pq\Pq\ell\nu$, and $4\Pq$.
With this algorithm, he and his students have conducted searches for $\PH\PH\to4\PQb$~[\mycite{CMS:2026znu}], $\PH\PH\to\bbbar 4\Pq$~[\mycite{CMS-PAS-HIG-23-012}], $\PX\to\PH\PY\to\bbbar 4\Pq$~[\mycite{CMS:2026shw}], and $\PH\to\Pq\Pq\ell\nu$~[\mycite{CMS:2026yrb}].
His graduate student Billy Li also led the development of an ML algorithm to solve the combinatorial problem of assigning jets to Higgs boson candidates~[\mycite{Li:2024qfq}], which was applied to the first CMS search for triple Higgs boson production~[\mycite{CMS-PAS-HIG-24-012}].

To enable these physics results, Duarte is a leader in the interdisciplinary subfield of ML for particle physics.
Rather than being explicitly programmed, ML algorithms learn directly from data and simulation to optimally perform a given task such as distinguishing Higgs bosons from background.
In the last review period, Duarte and collaborators applied novel ML algorithms to reconstruct particles from detector measurements in real proton-proton collisions at the LHC for the first time~[\mycite{CMS:2026znb}], track charged particles~[\mycite{Govil:2025nvy}, \mycite{Miao:2026yvl}], tag jets~[\mycite{Bhatnagar:2024fkx}, \mycite{Legge:2025cnm}, \mycite{Wang:2025dky}, \mycite{Wang:2026lqb}], and trigger on anomalies~[\mycite{CMS-DP-2024-059}, \mycite{CMS-DP-2025-061}].
Duarte also investigates methods for building ``foundation models'' in particle physics~[\mycite{Katel:2024ygn}, \mycite{Mokhtar:2025zqs}, \mycite{Hao:2025abk}, \mycite{Mokhtar:2026gdb}].
These results have been published in prominent particle physics journals like \emph{Phys. Rev. D}, \emph{Eur. Phys. J. C}, and \emph{Comm. Phys.} as well as the leading machine learning venues like the International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML) and \emph{Mach. Learn.: Sci. Technol.}.

Duarte also leads efforts to develop and implement ML algorithms in dedicated hardware for very fast processing, with a \emph{latency} or delay of less than one microsecond, to improve the real-time LHC event selection, known as the trigger.
As the trigger selects the subset of collision events to be preserved for analysis, while discarding the rest, it is of paramount importance.
% For this work, he designed, implemented, and benchmarked fast ML algorithms on field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs) for science~[\mycite{Weng:2023tailor}, \mycite{Weng:2024fkeras}, \mycite{Campos:2023pkp}, \mycite{McDermott:2023neural}, \mycite{Odagiu:2024bkp}].
Duarte co-created hls4ml, a compiler framework for deploying ML algorithms on FPGAs or ASICs that is widely used in particle physics and beoynd~[\mycite{Duarte:2018ite}, \mycite{Schulte:2025mai}].
With collaborators, he has also developed a framework for neural architecture codesign~[\mycite{Weitz:2025tcj}, \mycite{Weitz:2025dia}, \mycite{Weitz:2026xsv}], which uses a neural network surrogate model to predict hardware resources and latency~[\mycite{Hawks:2025hew}], and more efficient and robust hardware implementations of ML algorithms~[\mycite{Weng:2024fkeras}, \mycite{Weng:2025amigolut}].
The impact of this work is tremendous: by enabling sophisticated ML algorithms to run in the trigger, events that may contain subtle signatures of new physics may be identified and preserved rather than lost forever.

Duarte has made contributions to several other CMS searches for new physics involving long-lived particles, including a new search led by his posdoctoral researcher Daniel Diaz, which uses a unique data sample recorded and ``parked'' for later analysis by CMS in 2018~[\mycite{CMS:2025rtd}].
Duarte served as the co-convener of the Machine Learning group (2023--2025), where he oversaw the publication of novel ML methods in CMS, and now serves as the co-convener of the Higgs $\bbbar$/$\ccbar$ Subgroup (2025--2025).
As a member of the CMS Collaboration, he is also an author of all CMS publications since 2011, many of which benefit from his indirect contributions.

Duarte's research group currently includes two postdoctoral fellows and seven graduate students.
Duarte is a strong advocate for mentoring undergraduate researchers, with several of them having published papers and won department, university, and national awards.
Several of his trainees have gone on to begin successful careers in academia and industry, including prestigious Schmidt AI and CERN Fellowships.

Duarte received the APS Henry Primakoff Award for Early-Career Particle Physics, Sloan Research Fellowship (\$75,000), RCSA Cottrell Scholar Award (\$100,000), and Department of Energy (DOE) Early Career Award (ECA) for ``Real-Time Artificial Intelligence for Particle Reconstruction and Higgs Physics'' (\$750,000).
His group is also funded by the ``NSF Harnessing the Data Revolution (HDR) Institute for Accelerated AI Algorithms for Data Driven Discovery (A3D3)'' (\$15,000,000 total, \$675,600 for UCSD, 2021--2026) focused on the domains of multimessenger astronomy, neuroscience, and particle physics.
Duarte is also part of two training grants: ``High Energy Physics Consortium for Advanced Training (HEPCAT)'' (\$3,700,000 total, \$110,000 for UCSD so far, 2021--2026) and ``Western Advanced Training for Computational High-Energy Physics (WATCHEP)'' (\$2,000,000 total, \$440,00 for UCSD, 2022--2027).

Finally, he is a key personnel on a recent DOE award ``UCSD Experimental and Theoretical Particle Physics'' (\$6,928,000 total, \$3,915,000 for four faculty members in the UCSD CMS group, 2024--2028).

Duarte is committed to the educational mission of our institution, with a focus on modern computational research in physics.
He has developed a new ML and data science course for graduate and undergraduate students in the Physics Department, that is extremely popular, and revamped two existing upper-division computational physics courses.
His unique approach of simultaneously teaching students necessary computational skills, while also giving them the opportunity to explore open-ended research questions in collaboration fellow students, will provide a valuable introduction to ``authentic research collaboration.''
He has also co-written several definitive reviews, including one on ML for the Particle Data Group's 2026 Review of Particle Physics~[\mycite{Duarte:2025qbk}], which typically receiving thousands of citations.

Duarte is also a leader in equity, diversity, and inclusion (EDI) in our department and beyond.
For his efforts on equitable graduate admissions, he was awarded a UCSD Inclusive Excellence Award.
He is also one of the Physics representatives to the Mentoring Project, which aims to improve mentoring practices in the Physical Sciences Division.
He organized the APS Conference for Undergraduate Women and Gender Minorities in Physics (CU*iP) at UCSD in January 2025, which hosted more than 300 attendees from the region.
Beyond our department, he has helped established thriving programs for enhancing opportunities for underrepresented minority students in STEM: the HEPCAT (https://hepcat.ucsd.edu/), WATCHEP (https://watchep.org) and the NSF A3D3 Institute Postbaccalaurete Fellowship Program (https://a3d3.ai/).
He also created a database of URM scientists in physics and astronomy funded by the RSCA called ``Hidden Figures in Physics and Astronomy'' (\$25,000, 2023--2026).

Duarte has performed significant department-level and university-level service, including as the Physics faculty representative for the Mentoring Project in the Division of Graduate Education and Postdoctoral Affairs (GEPA), a member of the School of Physical Sciences Task Force on Data Science in the Physical Sciences, and a member of the Ad-Hoc Committee for the Five-Year Review of Warren College Provost Marisa Abrajano.

Phys. J. C}, \emph{Comput. Softw. Big Sci.}, \emph{Nucl. Instrum. Methods Phys. Res. A}, and \emph{Applied Optics}, and edited \emph{Front. Big Data} and \emph{Front. AI}.
He has also reviewed grant applications for the National Science Foundation, Department of Energy, European Science Foundation, and the French National Research Agency.
